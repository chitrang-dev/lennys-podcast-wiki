# Strategy Diagnosis and Choice

## Summary

The strategy episodes repeatedly argue that the hardest part of strategy is not generating ideas. It is correctly diagnosing the real challenge and then making a coherent set of choices instead of spreading effort across too many plausible options.

## Core Ideas

- Strategy begins with understanding the actual constraint, challenge, or opportunity.
- A diagnosis is useful because it narrows attention.
- Strategy requires choosing, not just analyzing.
- The quality of a strategy is inseparable from the quality of the choices it excludes.

## Key Syntheses

- `Diagnosis is the beginning of strategy`
  Richard Rumelt frames strategy around diagnosis, guiding policy, and coherent action.
- `Most teams underappreciate how much choice is required`
  Chandra Janakiraman and Ravi Mehta both emphasize that strategy is not a roadmap or a list of goals, but a forcing function on priorities.
- `You cannot answer strategy with only ambition`
  Roger Martin's choice cascade makes clear that aspiration alone is insufficient unless it is connected to concrete decisions.
- `The strategic problem is often misidentified`
  Teams frequently over-focus on solution shaping or execution while the real issue is still market, PMF, or where to focus.

## What Good Looks Like

- The core challenge is named clearly.
- The team can explain why this problem matters now.
- The strategy rules some options out.
- The chosen path is understandable and memorable.

## Failure Modes

- Confusing goals with strategy.
- Producing analysis without making choices.
- Letting every stakeholder preserve their favorite option.
- Treating prioritization as the same thing as strategy.
