# Product Strategy for Teams

## Summary

The strategy corpus is broad, but several episodes focus specifically on how strategy should work at the product or team level. The recurring message is that product strategy is not a mini version of corporate planning. It is the practical discipline of connecting user problems, strategic context, and a sequence of choices about what to build and why.

## Core Ideas

- Product strategy translates broader strategic logic into user and workflow choices.
- Product teams need strategy, not just delivery plans.
- PMF, customer understanding, and sequencing are part of product strategy.
- Team strategy should still answer what matters, for whom, and why now.

## Key Syntheses

- `Product strategy sits between mission and roadmap`
  Chandra Janakiraman provides the clearest articulation of this relationship.
- `PMF and product strategy are interdependent`
  Todd Jackson's PMF framing suggests many teams need to revisit persona, problem, promise, and product before they can claim to have a coherent strategy.
- `Product leaders need conviction under incomplete data`
  Ravi Mehta emphasizes informed conviction, especially in early-stage contexts where clean experimentation is limited.
- `Product teams should not default to feature accumulation`
  The strategy guests repeatedly imply that product strategy is as much about exclusion and sequence as invention.

## What Good Looks Like

- The team can explain the user problem and why it matters.
- The product roadmap reflects a strategic hypothesis, not a request backlog.
- Sequencing is intentional.
- Product decisions support a broader company or business strategy rather than drifting independently.

## Failure Modes

- Treating prioritization meetings as strategy work.
- Building features without a coherent theory of user value.
- Overweighting local data while ignoring broader strategic context.
- Using experimentation as a substitute for product judgment.
