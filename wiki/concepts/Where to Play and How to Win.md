# Where to Play and How to Win

## Summary

One of the strongest strategy patterns in the vault is that strategy is incomplete until it answers both where to compete and why this approach can win. This is broader than product scope. It includes markets, customer segments, positioning, differentiation, and the mechanism of advantage.

## Core Ideas

- Where to play defines the arena.
- How to win defines the advantage logic.
- Strategy is weaker when it only answers one of these.
- Differentiation and advantage need to be specific enough to shape resource allocation.

## Key Syntheses

- `Where to play is an explicit strategic choice`
  Roger Martin treats it as one of the core strategy questions, not a background assumption.
- `How to win requires a credible mechanism`
  Roger Martin and Hamilton Helmer both push against vague claims of being "better" without structural advantage.
- `Positioning and PMF can be strategic inputs`
  Todd Jackson's persona-problem-promise-product framing and several PMF episodes show that bad market selection and bad positioning often masquerade as execution problems.
- `Differentiation must be defended`
  The best strategy conversations in the vault connect market choice to capabilities, systems, and defensibility.

## What Good Looks Like

- A specific customer or market focus.
- A clear articulation of the basis for winning.
- Capabilities that reinforce the winning logic.
- A strategic posture that is legible to the organization.

## Failure Modes

- Choosing a market without a winning mechanism.
- Claiming differentiation that customers do not value.
- Trying to serve too many segments too early.
- Confusing broad TAM with good strategic focus.
