# Paid Growth and Distribution Channels

## Summary

The channel-focused growth episodes argue that distribution is dynamic, strategic, and increasingly competitive. Paid growth can be powerful, but it works best when treated as a system of channel-market fit, learning, and economics rather than as a volume dial.

## Core Ideas

- Distribution is often the difference between a strong product and a breakout company.
- Paid can be useful for almost every company, but not every company should expect paid to be its dominant engine.
- Channel behavior changes over time, so distribution strategy must be actively relearned.
- New platform shifts create temporary windows where challengers can move faster than incumbents.

## Key Syntheses

- `Every channel has its own user behavior`
  Timothy Davis emphasizes that channel experimentation needs platform-specific expectations rather than simple copy-paste creative.
- `Use signs-of-life tests before scaling`
  Small tests and existing data can show whether a platform has potential before serious spend.
- `The next distribution shift matters enormously`
  Brian Balfour frames new channels as escape-velocity opportunities in markets where product quality alone is not enough.
- `Paid is constrained by economics and product quality`
  Companies need enough margin, enough relevance, and enough clarity to make paid efficient.

## What Good Looks Like

- The team knows why a channel should work for this product.
- Small tests validate signs of life before scale.
- Channel strategy is tied to customer behavior and economics.
- Paid is integrated with broader growth learning rather than run as pure arbitrage.

## Failure Modes

- Assuming every platform should use the same creative or motion.
- Spending heavily before there is evidence of fit.
- Treating paid as the first answer to every growth question.
- Ignoring changing platform dynamics until the old playbook stops working.
