# Product-Market Fit and Activation

## Summary

The growth material in this vault repeatedly treats product-market fit as the non-negotiable foundation of durable growth. Activation and onboarding matter because they are the first real delivery of the product promise, not just because they are easy funnel levers.

## Core Ideas

- PMF is better understood as evidence of must-have value than as founder confidence.
- Activation is not only about smoothing friction. It is about getting the right user to the right value quickly.
- Onboarding is where market promise and product reality first meet.
- Teams often over-experiment on funnels when the real problem is still PMF or positioning.

## Key Syntheses

- `Find the users who truly care`
  Sean Ellis anchors PMF on identifying users who would be very disappointed without the product, then learning primarily from them.
- `PMF has stages`
  Todd Jackson's staged model suggests that PMF is not binary. Companies often need to know what stage they are in before choosing the right next move.
- `The first wave of growth learning belongs to the founders`
  Elena Verna argues that early PMF and distribution learning cannot be outsourced to a growth hire.
- `Onboarding is the highest-leverage universal experience`
  Adam Fishman emphasizes that onboarding is the only experience nearly every user touches, which makes it strategically important beyond simple conversion.

## What Good Looks Like

- The team has a concrete view of who the must-have user is.
- Activation metrics reflect real value moments, not vanity events.
- Onboarding reinforces the promise made in acquisition or positioning.
- PMF diagnosis changes the roadmap instead of sitting in a deck.

## Failure Modes

- Optimizing for "somewhat interested" users instead of must-have users.
- Treating onboarding as UI polish instead of value delivery.
- Hiring growth specialists before PMF is legible.
- Mistaking experimentation volume for progress.
