# Coaching and Feedback

## Summary

The coaching and feedback material in the vault pushes against a common managerial default: solving every problem yourself. Strong leaders use coaching, questions, and direct feedback to grow judgment in others rather than becoming the answer machine for the team.

## Core Ideas

- Coaching is not softness. It is leverage.
- Feedback should increase clarity, not just relieve the giver's discomfort.
- Difficult conversations work better when the goal is shared understanding rather than victory.
- Advising and coaching are different modes; strong managers know when to use each.

## Key Syntheses

- `Rescuing creates dependence`
  Rachel Lockett warns that leaders who always answer hard questions train their teams to escalate rather than think.
- `Good feedback raises the standard`
  Tobi Lütke frames feedback as reminding people of their actual potential, not just evaluating current output.
- `Influence requires diagnosis`
  Julie Zhuo shows that effective persuasion is not about being right louder, but understanding what will move a decision.
- `Trust improves the quality of hard conversations`
  Rachel Lockett and Carole Robin both point toward emotional honesty and relational safety as performance multipliers.

## What Good Looks Like

- Team members leave conversations with more clarity and more ownership.
- Feedback is specific enough to act on.
- Leaders can shift between coaching, advising, and deciding.
- Conflict produces better understanding and better choices.

## Failure Modes

- Solving every problem for the team.
- Delaying feedback until resentment builds.
- Treating difficult conversations as proof battles.
- Giving vague encouragement instead of usable truth.
