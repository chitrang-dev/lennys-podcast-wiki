# Product Strategy

## Summary

Across Lenny's Podcast, strategy is consistently framed as disciplined choice under uncertainty: understand the real challenge, decide where to play and how to win, and align resources behind a coherent set of actions instead of a grab bag of goals. The product lens matters, but the broader pattern applies equally to company strategy, business strategy, and team-level strategic choice.

## Core Pattern

- Strategy connects aspiration to action. It links mission or ambition to concrete choices about scope, capabilities, and execution.
- Good strategy starts with diagnosis, not brainstorming. The hard part is identifying the real challenge, constraint, or opportunity.
- Strategy requires answering both where to play and how to win, not just what to build.
- Strategy is fundamentally about tradeoffs. If everything is a priority, nothing is.
- A strategy only matters if execution, management systems, resourcing, and communication all reinforce it.

## Recurring Principles

- `Diagnose before deciding`
  Richard Rumelt frames strategy as a response to a high-stakes challenge, built from diagnosis, guiding policy, and coherent actions rather than vision slogans or goal lists.
- `Answer a full chain of choices`
  Roger Martin's strategy choice cascade broadens the frame: winning aspiration, where to play, how to win, required capabilities, and enabling management systems must fit together.
- `Force explicit choice`
  Chandra Janakiraman and Ravi Mehta both emphasize that strategy requires choosing where to focus, what horizon matters, and what not to do.
- `Winning requires either meaningful differentiation or structural advantage`
  Roger Martin repeatedly pushes against vague middle-ground positioning. A strategy needs a defensible logic for why you win.
- `Separate conviction from consensus theater`
  Several guests warn against pretending that alignment means everyone vaguely agrees. Real alignment requires shared clarity on the problem and the chosen path.
- `Use PMF and customer understanding as strategic anchors`
  Todd Jackson's PMF framing suggests that teams often get stuck because they change too little or optimize too early without revisiting persona, problem, promise, and product.
- `Keep strategy legible through systems`
  The strategy is weak if teams cannot explain why current work exists or if operating systems, decision rights, and capabilities do not reinforce the intended path.

## What Good Looks Like

- A small number of strategic choices that people can remember and act on.
- A clear explanation of the arena, the customer or user, the key challenge, and the logic for winning.
- A roadmap or operating plan that reflects the strategy instead of competing with it.
- Capabilities and management systems that actually support the chosen direction.
- Enough conviction to move, but enough humility to update when evidence changes.

## Common Failure Modes

- Mistaking goals, OKRs, or ambition statements for strategy.
- Treating the roadmap as strategy rather than as an output of strategy.
- Answering only one strategy question, such as aspiration or market, while leaving the rest implicit.
- Optimizing local teams or functions without a coherent company-level direction.
- Excessive experimentation when the issue is still basic positioning or PMF.
- Confusing extensive analysis with actual choice.

## Tensions To Manage

- `Broad business strategy vs. product-level strategy`
  The same principles recur at different levels, but the scope of choice changes. A company may choose markets and economic logic, while a product team may choose user problems and sequencing.
- `Long-term aspiration vs. near-term problem solving`
  Chandra distinguishes between a shorter-horizon, problem-forward strategy and a bigger aspirational view of the future.
- `Data vs. conviction`
  Ravi Mehta highlights that early-stage work often requires informed conviction because startup conditions do not always support classical experimentation.
- `Alignment vs. speed`
  Strong strategy is socially adoptable, but it cannot be watered down into vagueness just to create artificial consensus.
