# Growth Teams and Org Design

## Summary

The growth organization material in this vault is notably skeptical of premature specialization. Growth teams can be powerful, but only when the company already has enough product signal, data, and clarity to make the function useful rather than theatrical.

## Core Ideas

- Growth teams are not a shortcut to product-market fit.
- Founders and core product leaders need to own the first growth learning loops.
- A growth team is most useful when it can operate on real volume, real data, and a reasonably stable value proposition.
- Growth org design should match the company’s stage and bottleneck.

## Key Syntheses

- `Do not hire growth to solve pre-PMF uncertainty`
  Elena Verna argues that this is one of the most common mistakes companies make.
- `Growth requires specific competencies`
  Adam Fishman frames growth work as a distinct set of skills across product sense, experimentation, systems thinking, and execution.
- `The org should not isolate growth too early`
  Several episodes imply that if the whole company is not responsible for growth, the function can become an island with unrealistic expectations.
- `The right structure depends on the bottleneck`
  A strong growth team should be built around the actual problem: onboarding, retention, paid acquisition, pricing, loop design, or something else.

## What Good Looks Like

- The company knows why it needs a growth team now.
- Growth has access to usable data and enough user volume to learn.
- The function’s scope is defined by the real bottleneck.
- The team is integrated with product and not treated as a magic fix.

## Failure Modes

- Hiring a head of growth as a substitute for founder learning.
- Building a growth island that owns growth while everyone else ignores it.
- Using growth roles to hide strategy confusion.
- Defining the function so vaguely that it becomes accountable for everything and empowered for nothing.
