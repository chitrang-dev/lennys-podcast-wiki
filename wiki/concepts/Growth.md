# Growth

## Summary

The strongest recurring theme across growth episodes is that durable growth is downstream of product resonance, clear distribution mechanics, and system-level focus. Tactical hacks are repeatedly described as fragile, context-dependent, or actively misleading.

## Core Pattern

- Growth is not a substitute for product-market fit.
- The best growth systems compound because they improve activation, retention, and word of mouth together.
- Distribution matters as much as product quality, but channel advantage changes over time and must be re-learned.
- Teams that treat growth as a narrow function too early often avoid the harder work of PMF and distribution discovery.

## Recurring Principles

- `Find must-have value first`
  Sean Ellis centers growth on identifying users who would be very disappointed without the product, then learning from those users instead of pleasing everyone.
- `Do not outsource early growth learning`
  Elena Verna argues that founders must own the first wave of growth because PMF and distribution discovery cannot be delegated to a newly hired growth team.
- `Optimize for recommendation, not only conversion`
  Nilan Peiris shows that products earn word of mouth when they create experiences customers genuinely want to talk about, not just slightly smoother funnels.
- `New distribution platforms create breakout windows`
  Brian Balfour frames growth as a race to exploit emerging distribution shifts before incumbents adapt.
- `Growth systems need clear feedback loops`
  Shishir Mehrotra's loop framing and Sean Ellis's retention and activation focus both point toward growth as a set of compounding loops rather than a list of isolated experiments.

## What Good Looks Like

- PMF is measured with behavioral evidence, not just optimism.
- The company knows which growth loop matters most right now.
- Activation and onboarding are tied to core value, not just prettier flows.
- Growth work is rooted in actual user behavior and economics.
- Teams know when to place a conviction bet and when to run an experiment.

## Common Failure Modes

- Hiring a growth team before the company has PMF or useful data.
- Treating redesigns, rebrands, or generic hacks as growth strategy.
- Chasing acquisition while ignoring retention or recommendation.
- Letting experiments replace judgment instead of sharpening it.
- Assuming distribution is static when platforms and customer behavior are shifting.

## Tensions To Manage

- `Growth as product problem vs. growth as channel problem`
  The best episodes suggest it is almost always both.
- `Experimentation vs. strategic commitment`
  Growth teams need rigor, but over-experimentation can become a form of paralysis.
- `Word of mouth vs. paid channels`
  Wise-like compounding word of mouth is extraordinarily powerful, but not every product can rely on it equally.
