# Roger Martin on Crafting a Winning Strategy

## Question

What does Roger Martin's episode add to the vault's understanding of strategy, and how should a reader apply it when crafting a winning strategy?

## Answer

Roger Martin's episode sharpens the wiki's strategy synthesis in one important way: strategy is not a vision statement, a set of goals, or a pile of initiatives. It is an integrated set of choices that is designed to compel desired customer action. The center of gravity is not internal planning discipline by itself. The center of gravity is whether the chosen system of decisions gives customers a strong enough reason to choose you over alternatives.

His five-question cascade gives strategy a practical structure:

- `Winning aspiration`
  What are you trying to achieve, and what does winning actually mean?
- `Where to play`
  In which arena, customer, segment, use case, geography, or channel will you compete?
- `How to win`
  Why will customers choose you there instead of choosing a competitor or doing nothing?
- `Required capabilities`
  What must you be distinctly good at to make that win logic real?
- `Enabling management systems`
  What hiring, budgeting, planning, metrics, incentives, and operating rhythms are required to support those capabilities?

The most useful contribution is that these are not independent prompts. They form a chain. A vague aspiration leads to a fuzzy playing field. A fuzzy playing field leads to a weak win logic. A weak win logic makes capabilities generic. Generic capabilities create copyable strategies. In Martin's framing, strength comes from fit across the whole chain.

## High-Signal Synthesis

- `Strategy is choice, not aspiration theater`
  Martin is unusually explicit that strategy only becomes real once leaders choose a specific place to play and a specific logic for winning. This aligns with [[wiki/concepts/Strategy Diagnosis and Choice]], but Martin adds a more complete operating sequence than most guests.
- `The job of strategy is to influence customer behavior`
  His definition pushes the vault beyond internal planning language. A strategy matters only if it changes what customers do.
- `How to win must be concrete`
  Martin repeatedly rejects vague claims like "we will be the best" or "we will innovate." A strategy needs a reason customers will perceive you as superior, cheaper, easier, safer, faster, or otherwise more compelling.
- `Capabilities and systems are part of strategy, not downstream execution detail`
  This is one of the episode's strongest lessons. Many teams stop after defining a target market and a product thesis. Martin insists that the operating model required to deliver the promise is part of the strategy itself.
- `Durable advantage is still possible`
  He argues against the fashionable belief that all advantage is fleeting. The issue is not whether moats can last. The issue is whether the system of choices is hard enough to replicate that competitors decide the overlap is not worth the pain.
- `Customer reality beats incumbent preference`
  His examples about Amazon, ETFs, and AI search reinforce that strategy must follow where customers are actually going rather than defending legacy economics indefinitely.

  Martin makes this page more rigorous by treating management systems as a first-class part of strategy rather than mere implementation machinery.

  The episode broadens the page from product strategy toward general strategy. It shows how company strategy, business strategy, and product strategy share the same logic even when the level of choice changes.

  It clarifies where product teams fit: they usually inherit part of the where-to-play from company strategy, but still need a sharp answer for user, problem, sequence, and win logic.

## Representative Examples From The Episode

- `Four Seasons`
  The core move was not "offer luxury." It was define luxury around making business travelers feel more at home and more effective than they would in a standard luxury hotel. That required different frontline talent and different management systems.
- `Southwest`
  The moat came from a tightly connected operating model, not one tactic. Competitors could imitate fragments, but copying the whole system was painful enough that most attempts produced bad imitations.
- `Olay vs. Clinique`
  Martin's example shows how channel conflict can create a fault line that prevents incumbents from following an attacker into a new position even when they understand the threat.
- `Google and AI search`
  His point is not that incumbents should surrender. It is that customer behavior eventually overpowers leaders who spend too long protecting yesterday's profit pool.

## Implications For Readers

- Start strategy work by forcing sharper choices, not by generating longer decks.
- Treat "where to play" as a boundary-setting decision. It should clarify who the strategy is not for.
- Demand a specific answer to "why will we win here?" If the answer sounds interchangeable, it is not strategy yet.
- Design capabilities and management systems early. If they are left vague, the strategy is probably still wishful thinking.
- Revisit the chain when markets shift. A change in customer behavior can invalidate the current where, how, or system.
- Build strategy skill by repetition. Martin is clear that strong strategists are usually practiced, not naturally gifted.
